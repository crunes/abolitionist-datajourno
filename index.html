Towards an Abolitionist Data Journalism Practice

This website is an ongoing project by Charmaine Runes, a Chicago-based developer, designer, and journalist. This micro-site was born out of generous conversations with other (data) journalists, abolitionist friends, as well as classmates for a course on prison industrial complex abolition. It explores and builds on a question she has had for a while and will likely think about for the rest of her life:

What might an abolitionist data journalism practice look like?



But first, a few definitions:

What is the prison industrial complex?

This project uses Critical Resistance’s definition of the prison industrial complex (PIC), which is “the overlapping interests of government and industry that use surveillance, policing, and imprisonment as solutions to economic, social and political problems.” The PIC encompasses not only police departments and prisons, but also carceral approaches to child welfare, schools, and social work. The PIC manifests at every scale, from the interpersonal to the structural.

What is abolition?

Critical Resistance defines abolition as “a political vision with the goal of eliminating imprisonment, policing, and surveillance and creating lasting alternatives to punishment and imprisonment.” PIC abolition is doing away with cages and creating conditions that prevent violence. It is both the horizon and the small actions we take every day to build another world, the kind where no one is disposable and everyone has what they need to grow and thrive.

What is data journalism?

Data journalism is simply journalism based on collecting, analyzing, and interpreting data. Although the term is relatively new, journalists have been using data for their reporting for a long time. It often (but not always) involves visualizing data using charts, and using the data to provide deeper context to a story and improve its accuracy. And while data journalism is used in newsrooms all over the world, this project mostly focuses on data journalism in the United States.

Context

An incomplete history of data journalism

One of the earliest records of data journalism in the United States was an 1849 story and chart by the New York Tribune, illustrating the number of people who had died from cholera. In 1858, across the Atlantic, Florence Nightingale presented her now-famous coxcomb diagrams to describe the different causes of death for the British Army.


The New York Tribune's 1849 cholera visualization. (Image via ProPublica)


Clearly, data journalism is not new, but what distinguishes these simple—but powerful—tabulations from modern-day data journalism is the sheer speed and scale at which we can generate, access, and analyze data today using computers. In 1952, CBS News covered the presidential election night using a Remington Rand UNIVAC computer, correctly predicting Eisenhower’s landslide victory. And in 1967, Philip Meyer (at the time a freelance journalist with the Detroit Free Press) used survey research methods to report on the Detroit uprisings that summer and later, the civil rights and anti-war movements. Meyer called this computer-assisted approach to reporting “precision journalism”.

As computers grew faster and cheaper, more and more newsrooms began using them for their reporting. In 1989, the Atlanta Journal-Constitution received a Pulitzer Prize for its computer-assisted reporting on racial disparities in home loan practices. That same year, the Missouri School of Journalism and the Investigative Reporters and Editors Inc. established the National Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting (NICAR) to support and train journalists to use data for their investigative work. Today, large legacy newsrooms like the New York Times have entire desks and news products like The Upshot dedicated to collecting, cleaning, and making sense of massive amounts of data.


Data + journalism + the myth of objectivity

Names like computer-assisted reporting, precision journalism, and data journalism might suggest that this kind of reporting is more accurate and more objective than other forms. But is it?

What does data have to do with journalistic objectivity?

In the 19th century, newspapers were often openly aligned with specific political parties and issues, including enslavement and abolition. But writing and running stories that exposed state violence and condemned the country’s white supremacist status quo resulted in threats and physical harm against reporters and editors that did so.

In 1829, David Walker, a Black abolitionist and Boston agent for Freedom’s Journal (the country’s first newspaper that was both Black-owned and managed), wrote a pamphlet that used the Bible and the Declaration of Independence to argue against enslavement and encourage other Black people to fight for their freedom. Soon after he distributed his pamphlets, there was a $1,000 reward for his death and a $10,000 reward if he was captured alive. In 1837, Elijah P. Lovejoy, a white Presbyterian editor, was killed in Alton, IL, for publishing anti-enslavement literature in his newspaper.

But towards the start of the 20th century, newsrooms dropped their political patrons in favor of advertisers. Relying on advertisements meant prioritizing circulation and reaching as many people as possible. A parallel force behind the push for nonpartisan media was the closing or merging of several newsrooms across the country in the 1920s, which encouraged the remaining shops to appeal to the broadest swath of the population.

In the 1940s, the mainstream journalist’s job was very similar to that of a stenographer; they simply reported what powerful people said and did, with little commentary or critique. But by the time the 1960s came around, reporters covering then-President McCarthy’s outrageous claims pushed back.

It was not enough to report “the facts”; journalists and newsrooms were also responsible for telling the truth about the facts.

The centrality of facts and the discipline of verification became of utmost importance; so too was the idea that journalists be “objective” and their reporting dispassionate and disinterested. Reporters were expected to portray issues and events in a neutral way, regardless of their personal feelings. This standard has also meant that reporters with lived experience are discouraged or prevented from writing about their communities because their reporting would be too biased. And although many reporters and scholars have offered new paradigms for journalistic truth-telling, several—particularly marginalized journalists—have faced harsh consequences.

Lewis Raven Wallace, author of The View From Somewhere: Undoing the Myth of Journalistic Objectivity, argues that “objectivity” has been used to silence marginalized reporters (himself included), as far back as data journalist Ida B. Wells-Barnett. Wells-Barnett collected and used data to expose and protest the horrors of lynching. In 1895, she published a pamphlet called The Red Record: Tabulated Statistics and Alleged Causes of Lynching in the United States. She also wrote an article, Lynching and the Excuse for it, as a scathing response to Jane Addams. Addams was against lynching, but she had argued that lynching spectators might “honestly believe that that is the only successful method of dealing with a certain class of crimes”—that is, Black men charged with raping white women—and should be given the benefit of the doubt.

But Addams’ generosity was based on the false assumption and widely-held white public perception that the majority of those lynched had been charged with that specific crime in the first place—and Wells-Barnett had the facts to prove it. She drew up multiple tables tallying the number of Black people who were lynched and the justification by year, from 1896 to 1900. The percent of Black people whom white mobs lynched for a rape charge was the lowest category each year. In 1900, less than fifteen percent of those lynched were charged with rape.

“The table tells its own story, and shows how false is the excuse which lynchers offer to justify their fiendishness,” Wells-Barnett concluded.

The idea that data talks is a popular one; so too is the idea that data (especially quantitative data) is neutral, “objective”, and consequently, authoritative. But Rob Kitchin, author of The Data Revolution: Big Data, Open Data, Data Infrastructures & Their Consequences, provides the following definition of data: “what we understand as data are actually… those units of data that have been selected and harvested from the sum of all potential data.”

Data, he continues, are “inherently partial, selective, and representative, and the distinguishing criteria used in their capture has consequence.”

In other words, data—be it qualitative or quantitative, structured or unstructured, primary or secondary—is constructed. Datasets represent not only categories, but also the specific choices of the dataset’s creator. And at every stage of data journalism, reporters make similarly subjective choices: what data to use, what variables to analyze, how to “clean” a dataset, how to visualize a trend, what data points to highlight, and what language they use to explain what the data mean.

Data doesn’t talk; the humans who make sense of the data do.


Data journalism + reimagining the world

Data and data journalism often tells us what is, but it can also tell us what can be. Using data in investigative and watchdog journalism, for example, can help expose harm and provide critical, accurate information to those committed to and organizing for social change. Using data to simulate and explain policy changes that free people in cages, house people experiencing homelessness, educate children equitably, or redistribute wealth can also help readers imagine alternative ways of being in the world.

Seek out researchers and organizers experimenting with new ways to keep their communities safe.

Talk to mutual aid groups about the funds they’ve collected and distributed to their neighbors; ask them what impact looks like.

Show people that another world is, in fact, possible.

Praxis

Moving towards or building an abolitionist data journalism practice can look different for different people. Some might have the ability to join a news organization dedicated to movement or solidarity journalism, and others might not. But data journalism is first and foremost an activity and skill, not a profession, and skills can be applied and shared in many settings.

Here are some ways to consider building that practice, for yourself and in community:



Learn about prison industrial complex abolition

Read and listen to articles, books, blogs, and podcasts about PIC abolition by abolitionists. Reflect on carceral assumptions and narratives you’ve held on to yourself. Talk to others about what you’re learning and what questions you still have.


Avoid narratives + language that legitimize the prison industrial complex

When using data to illustrate the harms of the PIC, use language that recognizes the dignity of the people involved. Avoid narratives that frame prisons or police as fix-able given enough financial resources and time.


Understand what + who your data represent

Crime data, like the kind the City of Chicago releases, is easy to access. The City website notes that it pulls 90 days’ worth of data from the Chicago Police Department's (CPD) CLEAR (Citizen Law Enforcement Analysis and Reporting) system. CPD includes the following disclaimer:

These crimes are based upon preliminary information supplied to the Police Department by the reporting parties and have not been verified. The preliminary crime classifications may be changed at a later date based upon additional investigation and there is always the possibility of mechanical or human error. Additionally, crimes not reported within 97 days of occurrence would be excluded from this dataset. Therefore, the Chicago Police Department does not guarantee (either expressed or implied) the accuracy, completeness, timeliness, or correct sequencing of the information and the information should not be used for comparison purposes over time. The Chicago Police Department will not be responsible for any error or omission, or for the use of, or the results obtained from the use of this information.

Some questions you can ask before you work with a dataset:

  • Who collected these data, and for what purpose?
  • What makes this a legitimate source?
  • What critical questions do this data NOT answer? What data are missing?

For example, if you’re working with murder registry data, ask yourself, “Do these data represent murderers, or do they represent people who have been convicted of murder?”

And remember, data about an issue is not inherently more valuable, trustworthy, or true than information gleaned through interviews with those directly impacted or personal experience.


Connect the dots

In her 1982 Malcolm X weekend address at Harvard University, Audre Lorde said:

“There is no such thing as a single-issue struggle, because we do not live single-issue lives.”

Similarly, there is no such thing as a single-issue story, and an abolitionist data journalism practice should reflect that.

The #NoCopAcademy campaign in Chicago, IL, for example, was more than just a fight to prevent the construction of a $90 million police training academy on the West Side. It was also about historical disinvestment by the city to West Side neighborhoods, as well as the Black and brown youth building power and community in spite of it. As Benji Hart, Chicago-based organizer, put it, “The police beat is the housing beat is the education beat.”


Share skills + resources beyond your profession

If you are not in a position to or decide not to make your living from movement journalism, build relationships with others who you can continue to learn from. Organize within your newsroom. Connect with organizers and campaigns and see how you can support their work.

As Darryl Holliday wrote for the Columbia Journalism Review:

“A healthy and democratic future demands that the skills journalists possess be distributed among many—not bestowed on a select few. The journalists we need today are not heroic observers of crisis—they are conveners, facilitators, organizers, educators, on-demand investigators, and community builders. Most of all, they strengthen the systems that make communities resilient.”
